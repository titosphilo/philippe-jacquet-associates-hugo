# EMDR Therapy London

Eye Movement Desensitisation and Reprocessing (EMDR) is one of the most effective therapies available for trauma, PTSD, anxiety, and a range of psychological difficulties where distressing memories remain unprocessed. It is recommended by the NHS, the World Health Organization, and the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) as a first-line treatment for post-traumatic stress disorder.

Dr Philippe Jacquet has been practising EMDR for over 23 years — since the early years of its clinical use in the United Kingdom, before formal accreditation structures existed. That depth of experience shapes the way EMDR is integrated here: not as a standalone protocol, but woven into a wider psychotherapeutic relationship that draws on integrative, Jungian, and psychodynamic thinking.

## What EMDR Does

EMDR works by engaging the brain's natural information-processing system. Distressing experiences — particularly those involving shock, loss, abuse, or prolonged stress — are sometimes stored in a fragmented way that keeps them emotionally live. A smell, a sound, a situation can re-activate the original distress as if it were happening now.

Through guided bilateral stimulation (typically eye movements, but also tapping or auditory tones), EMDR enables the brain to reprocess these memories so they lose their emotional charge. The memory does not disappear — but it becomes something that can be recalled without the body re-entering a state of alarm.

Most clients are surprised that EMDR does not require them to describe traumatic experiences in detail. The processing happens largely internally, with the therapist guiding the pace and ensuring the session remains within a manageable emotional window.

## What EMDR Treats

EMDR is effective across a wide range of presentations:

**Trauma and PTSD** — single-incident trauma (accidents, assault, medical procedures) and complex or developmental trauma (prolonged childhood adversity, emotional neglect, relational abuse).

**Anxiety and phobias** — where anxiety is rooted in a specific memory or cluster of experiences rather than generalised worry.

**Eating disorders** — Dr Jacquet holds a Doctorate of Professional Practice specifically in male eating disorders and has worked extensively on the traumatic underpinnings of restrictive and compulsive eating. EMDR is increasingly recognised as a valuable component of eating disorder treatment.

**Addiction and compulsive behaviour** — many addictive patterns are maintained by unprocessed emotional states. EMDR can address the underlying experiences that drive the compulsion, alongside other therapeutic work.

**Grief and loss** — particularly where bereavement has become complicated or frozen.

**Performance anxiety** — in clinical, professional, and sporting contexts.

## How Sessions Work

An EMDR course of treatment begins with a thorough assessment and history-taking. Before any reprocessing begins, time is spent building the resources and stabilisation skills that make the work safe and effective. This preparatory phase varies in length depending on the complexity of the presentation.

Reprocessing sessions typically run for the standard 50-minute hour, though extended sessions can be arranged for intensive work. The number of sessions required depends on the nature and complexity of the material — single-incident trauma can sometimes resolve in three to six sessions; complex or developmental trauma generally requires a longer course of work.

## EMDR Within a Broader Therapeutic Relationship

What distinguishes the work here from a purely protocol-based EMDR service is the integration of depth and psychodynamic thinking. EMDR identifies and processes specific memory nodes — but the patterns that led to vulnerability, and the meaning that difficult experiences have acquired over a lifetime, often require a richer conversation.

For many clients, EMDR forms one phase of a longer therapeutic engagement. For others, a focused EMDR course is sufficient. The approach is tailored to the individual and reviewed as the work progresses.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How many EMDR sessions will I need?**
This depends significantly on the nature of the difficulty. Single-incident trauma (one clear event) typically resolves in three to eight sessions. Complex trauma — particularly where difficulties began in childhood — generally requires a longer course of work, sometimes integrated with ongoing psychotherapy. An initial assessment gives a clearer picture.

**Is EMDR better than CBT for trauma?**
Both are recommended by NICE for PTSD. The evidence base for EMDR is strong, particularly for single-incident trauma and situations where the client finds it difficult to talk about what happened. EMDR does not require detailed verbal description of traumatic material, which many people find a significant advantage.

**Can EMDR be done online?**
Yes. Online EMDR is clinically effective and is offered via secure video. Bilateral stimulation is adapted for the online format. Many clients work online throughout their entire course of treatment.

**Is EMDR suitable for complex or developmental trauma?**
Yes, though it requires careful preparation. Complex trauma — particularly involving early relational experiences — benefits from a period of stabilisation and resource-building before reprocessing begins. The work moves at a pace that is safe and appropriate for the individual.

**What does an EMDR session feel like?**
Most clients describe EMDR as more experiential than talking therapy — less verbal, more internal. Sessions can be emotionally significant, and it is normal to continue processing between appointments. The therapist works to ensure the session ends with sufficient grounding and stabilisation.
